## 1. Strategic Insights

### The Market Opportunity
- "Whisper" and "Voice" are **burned** — 7+ and 6+ apps respectively
- Most on-device competitors have functional names (BetterDictation, VoiceInk) or whisper derivatives
- Almost NO competitor has a strong, abstract, memorable brand name
- A distinctive brand name for a consumer on-device dictation app would be genuinely differentiated

### App Store Strategy
- Abstract/branded names + keyword-rich subtitles outperform keyword-stuffed names
- You do NOT need "dictation," "voice," or "whisper" in the app name
- Optimal format: `[Brand Name] — AI Voice Dictation & Polish` (subtitle carries keywords)
- Never duplicate keywords across title and subtitle — Apple only counts each once

### Domain Strategy
- `.app` is the best alternative to `.com` (Bear switched TO .app from .com)
- Avoid prefix workarounds (get-, try-, use-) which fragment brand recall
- `.ai` is strong for AI-positioned products
- Avoid hyphens in domains

### The Three-Way Tension
Every name optimizes differently across three axes:

| Axis | What It Means | Names That Win |
|------|--------------|---------------|
| **Voice/Speech semantics** | Name evokes speaking, voice, sound | Lilt, Verse, Mouthy, Chops, Pithy |
| **Visual/Spectrum semantics** | Name connects to rainbow icon | Chroma, Shimmer, Speaktra, Lustre |
| **Premium polish semantics** | Name evokes LLM refinement step | Lustre, Gloss, Pithy, Hone |

- **Icon fit:** The lips icon IS mouthy — literally a mouth being mouthy
- **"I use Mouthy for dictation"** — Cheeky, self-deprecating, fun
- **"Mouthy by Envious"** — Playful meets premium. The contrast works.
- **Conflict:** Old defunct Mouthy chat app (2013). Very low conflict.
- **Why #1:** It IS the founder's insight distilled into a single word. The icon is a mouth. The app listens to mouths. Being mouthy is the feature, not the bug. The "criticism-as-feature" angle is genuinely clever brand territory.

#### #2: CHOPS
- **Evokes:** "Having the chops" = having skill. Also: jaw/mouth area. Musical chops = technical ability.
- **Icon fit:** The lip area IS the chops — and the app gives your words professional chops
- **"I use Chops for dictation"** — Natural, confident. Implies mastery.
- **"Chops by Envious"** — Feels like a pro tool with personality.
- **Conflict:** Drum rudiment app, food ordering. Low conflict in dictation space.
- **Why #2:** The positive double meaning (mouth + skill) is rare. Most mouth words are slightly negative. Chops is aspirational.

#### #3: PITHY
- **Evokes:** Concise, forcefully expressive. The app takes your rambling speech and makes it pithy.
- **Icon fit:** The lips deliver pithy statements — brief but powerful
- **"I use Pithy for dictation"** — Clean, smart. Writers would love this.
- **"Pithy by Envious"** — Intellectual meets aspirational.
- **Conflict:** Pithy Reader, Pithy Digital agency. None in dictation.
- **Why #3:** The LLM polishing step literally makes text more pithy. The name IS the value prop.

#### #4: GOB
- **Evokes:** British slang for mouth ("shut your gob"). Punchy, irreverent.
- **Icon fit:** Rainbow lips are literally a gob — open, expressive
- **"I use Gob for dictation"** — Short, distinctive, slightly British-cheeky.
- **"Gob by Envious"** — Compact, memorable.
- **Conflict:** Less common as software name. No conflicts found.
- **Why #4:** Brevity and distinctiveness are remarkable. Risk: Americans might not get it. But "Git" proved the concept.

#### #5: KISSER
- **Evokes:** Slang for mouth ("shut your kisser"). Playful, old-timey cool.
- **Icon fit:** Rainbow lips are literally a kisser. Perfect visual match.
- **"I use Kisser for dictation"** — Cheeky, memorable, conversation-starter.
- **"Kisser by Envious"** — Fun but risks feeling too casual.
- **Conflict:** Only prank/game apps. Low conflict for dictation.
- **Why #5:** Maximum personality and memorability. Riskier than top 4 — some professionals might hesitate.
